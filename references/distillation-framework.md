# Distillation Framework

Use this framework to turn a thinker into a teaching lens without turning the Skill into impersonation.

## Goal

Extract repeatable intellectual moves that improve learning:

- worldview or intellectual posture
- reasoning pattern
- teaching style
- explanation structure
- questioning method
- preferred examples and representations
- likely emphasis and likely omissions
- suitable and weak subject contexts
- characteristic failure risks

Distill thinking, not personality. A lens is a modern pedagogical synthesis informed by public sources; it is not a reconstruction of the person's private mind or literal answer to a modern question.

## Source standard

Use sources in this order when possible:

1. Primary or near-primary works such as lectures, published writings, and historically attributed dialogues.
2. Reputable scholarly histories, biographies, and critical editions.
3. High-quality secondary explanations that identify their evidence.

For each claim:

- distinguish a documented method from an interpretive teaching adaptation;
- name source works or traditions without inventing page-level precision;
- avoid unattributed aphorisms and quote collections;
- avoid long quotations, even when the work is public domain;
- state historical uncertainty, mediation, or disputed authorship where relevant;
- do not claim that a thinker held a position on a subject that postdates them.

## Distillation process

1. **Collect.** Identify recurring reasoning and teaching moves across more than one reliable source when available.
2. **Separate.** Remove costume, accent, biography trivia, verbal tics, and unsupported legends.
3. **Translate.** Convert each recurring move into an actionable instruction for teaching a modern topic.
4. **Bound.** Record where the move is strong, where it is weak, and how it can distort a concept.
5. **Test.** Apply the lens to at least one fitting and one awkward topic. Check academic correctness first.
6. **Document.** Use the standard template below and label synthesis as synthesis.

## Standard thinker template

```markdown
# [Thinker] Lens

## Source basis
## Core worldview
## Thinking pattern
## Teaching style
## Explanation method
## Questioning method
## Best subjects
## Weak subjects
## Trigger phrases
## Response structure
## Example response
## Safety boundaries
```

## Section guidance

### Source basis

List a small, defensible source foundation. Note whether access to the thinker is direct, mediated by students or later writers, historically uncertain, or interpreted for modern teaching.

### Core worldview

Describe only the intellectual posture that affects explanation. Avoid totalizing claims about the person's identity or beliefs.

### Thinking pattern

Express the lens as operations an assistant can perform: simplify, decompose, define, contrast, test, reverse, model, or synthesize.

### Teaching style and explanation method

Specify the order of moves, the kind of examples to prefer, when to introduce notation, and how to reconnect the lens to standard academic language.

### Questioning method

Define the purpose and maximum useful number of questions per turn. Questions should diagnose or advance understanding, not merely sound characteristic.

### Best and weak subjects

Describe fit as a gradient, not a prohibition. State the adaptation required in weak contexts.

### Trigger phrases

Include direct names in English and Chinese plus distinctive method requests. Do not treat broad subject names as decisive triggers.

### Response structure

Provide a short, repeatable sequence that still permits adaptation to user intent.

### Example response

Demonstrate the method on a small topic. Do not write invented dialogue or fake quotation.

### Safety boundaries

Record lens-specific risks in addition to the shared [safety boundaries](safety-boundaries.md).

## Acceptance checklist

A lens is ready only when all answers are “yes”:

- Does every major method have a defensible public basis or an explicit synthesis label?
- Can another agent apply the lens without copying tone or pretending to be the thinker?
- Does the lens change the reasoning structure in observable ways?
- Are best-fit and weak-fit contexts both documented?
- Does the example teach a correct academic concept?
- Are quotations absent or brief, verified, necessary, and attributed?
- Are lens-specific failure modes and recovery moves explicit?
- Is the lens meaningfully different from the other three V0.1 lenses?

## Version 0.1 constraint

Maintain only Feynman, von Neumann, Socrates, and Laozi. Evaluate any future proposal with this framework, examples, and the quality rubric before expanding scope.
