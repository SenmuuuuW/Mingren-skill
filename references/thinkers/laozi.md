# Laozi

## Scope

This file derives bounded systems questions from the *Daodejing* textual tradition. It does not reproduce a mystical voice, resolve authorship debates, or treat non-forcing as passivity.

## Core Lens

Examine relations, feedback, change, and unintended effects; seek the minimum effective intervention that preserves safety and lets the system carry useful work.

## How They Explain

### Explain a term through its relations

- **Source evidence:** Relational and contrastive patterns throughout the *Daodejing*; exact passage mapping remains required per rule.
- **Original context:** Aphoristic discussions of naming, conduct, governance, and change.
- **Method summary:** Describe how a thing depends on, contrasts with, and changes alongside other things.
- **Underlying purpose:** Avoid treating context-dependent categories as isolated essences.
- **Applicable situations:** Feedback systems, relational concepts, organizational behavior, tradeoffs.
- **Non-applicable situations:** Fixed technical definitions or urgent instructions needing unambiguous direct action.
- **Executable rule:** Name relevant counterparts and dependencies; describe how changing one alters the others; retain concrete mechanisms.
- **Correct example:** Explain “efficiency” relative to workload, reliability, and recovery cost.
- **Failure example:** Say everything is connected without naming a causal relation.
- **Confidence:** provisional

## How They Ask Questions

### Inspect reversal and second-order effects

- **Source evidence:** Recurring reversal and governance themes in the *Daodejing*; passage-level verification pending.
- **Original context:** Claims about excess, control, value, and governance.
- **Method summary:** Ask how an intervention changes incentives, adaptation, and later system state.
- **Underlying purpose:** Detect outcomes that oppose the intervention's first-order aim.
- **Applicable situations:** Policy, product incentives, organizational controls, complex adaptive systems.
- **Non-applicable situations:** Immediate emergency response before established safeguards are applied.
- **Executable rule:** State the desired first-order effect; trace one feedback loop and one plausible adaptation; add a monitoring threshold.
- **Correct example:** Before adding alerts, examine alert fatigue and define a removal threshold.
- **Failure example:** Invoke “unintended consequences” as a reason never to act.
- **Confidence:** provisional

## How They Simplify or Structure Complex Problems

### Choose minimum effective intervention

- **Source evidence:** Themes conventionally associated with `wuwei` in the *Daodejing*; exact operational generalization requires scholarship.
- **Original context:** Conduct and governance, not modern incident response or engineering doctrine.
- **Method summary:** Remove unnecessary control and choose the least force that reliably achieves the aim.
- **Underlying purpose:** Reduce brittleness, resistance, and intervention-created work.
- **Applicable situations:** Reversible product/process changes and systems with useful self-correction.
- **Non-applicable situations:** Emergencies, abuse, security incidents, or any domain with mandatory controls.
- **Executable rule:** Establish safety floor; compare no action, smallest reversible action, and stronger action; choose the smallest meeting success criteria; monitor and escalate.
- **Correct example:** Trial one reversible form-field removal and measure completion and error rates.
- **Failure example:** Leave a known vulnerability unpatched because intervention might disturb the system.
- **Confidence:** medium

### Model change rather than static labels

- **Source evidence:** Change and reversal motifs in the *Daodejing*; exact method attribution is interpretive.
- **Original context:** Aphoristic observations about processes and extremes.
- **Method summary:** Describe states, transitions, delays, and feedback instead of assigning a permanent label.
- **Underlying purpose:** Prevent a snapshot from being mistaken for stable behavior.
- **Applicable situations:** Trends, adaptive behavior, lifecycle decisions.
- **Non-applicable situations:** Stable classifications defined by specification.
- **Executable rule:** Identify current state, direction, driver, delay, and possible reversal condition.
- **Correct example:** Analyze declining errors alongside traffic, reporting delay, and threshold changes.
- **Failure example:** Replace measurement with vague claims that change is inevitable.
- **Confidence:** provisional

## Recurring Principles

- Interventions can reshape the system that receives them (provisional).
- Opposites and categories may be mutually defining rather than independent (provisional).
- Restraint is active calibration, not default passivity (medium interpretation; source mapping needed).

## Trigger Candidates

### Intervention may create feedback or resistance

- **Trigger condition:** A proposed control affects incentives, adaptation, or a dynamic system.
- **Primary lens:** Laozi
- **Optional secondary lens:** von Neumann for explicit states and interfaces
- **Actions:** establish safety floor; map first- and second-order effects; compare intervention sizes; select a reversible minimum; monitor escalation criteria.
- **Avoid:** mystical wording, passive delay, treating all opposites as equivalent, ignoring mandatory controls.
- **Exit condition:** A safe action, feedback measure, and escalation threshold are specified.
- **Confidence:** provisional

## Misconceptions to Avoid

- Non-forcing does not mean doing nothing.
- “Balance” is not an excuse to split the difference between truth and error.
- Aphorisms are not technical evidence, emergency procedures, or universal optimization rules.
